Building a Retirement Plan That Works

Retirement planning is one of the areas of personal finance where starting early makes an extraordinary difference. Thanks to compound growth, a 25-year-old who invests $6,000 per year in a tax-advantaged retirement account earning 7% annually will have roughly $1.4 million by age 65. The same person starting at 35 — just ten years later — would accumulate around $680,000, less than half as much, even though they contributed for 30 years instead of 20. Time in the market is the single most powerful variable in retirement saving, which is why the best time to start is always now.

The U.S. tax code offers several types of retirement accounts designed to encourage long-term saving, each with distinct rules around contributions, tax treatment, and withdrawals. Individual Retirement Accounts (IRAs) — both Roth and Traditional — are available to anyone with earned income and allow annual contributions up to $7,000 in 2026 ($8,000 if you're 50 or older). Employer-sponsored 401(k) plans allow much higher annual contributions — up to $23,500 in 2026, plus a $7,500 catch-up for those 50 and older — and often include employer matching contributions, which are essentially free money that dramatically boosts your effective savings rate.

The core strategic question for most savers is whether to prioritize pre-tax accounts (like Traditional IRAs and traditional 401(k)s) or after-tax accounts (like Roth IRAs and Roth 401(k)s). Pre-tax contributions reduce your taxable income today and grow tax-deferred, but withdrawals in retirement are taxed as ordinary income. Roth contributions are made with after-tax dollars, but qualified withdrawals in retirement — including all the growth — are completely tax-free. The right choice depends on your current tax rate, your expected tax rate in retirement, your time horizon, and your state's tax rules.

Retirement Account Basics

  • 401(k) employer match: Always contribute enough to capture the full employer match before anything else — a 50% or 100% match is an instant, guaranteed return that no investment can beat.
  • IRA contribution limits: In 2026, you can contribute up to $7,000 to an IRA ($8,000 if 50+), regardless of how many IRA accounts you have — the limit applies across all traditional and Roth IRAs combined.
  • Roth income limits: The ability to contribute directly to a Roth IRA phases out at higher income levels ($150,000–$165,000 for single filers in 2026). High earners can use the backdoor Roth strategy as an alternative.
  • Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs): Traditional IRA and 401(k) account holders must begin taking minimum withdrawals at age 73; Roth IRAs have no RMDs during the account owner's lifetime.
  • Asset allocation: Younger investors can typically afford more equity exposure; as you approach retirement, gradually shifting toward bonds and stable income sources reduces sequence-of-returns risk.
💡 The Retirement Savings Priority Order

A widely recommended approach: (1) Contribute enough to your 401(k) to capture the full employer match. (2) Max out an HSA if you have a high-deductible health plan — it offers triple tax advantages. (3) Max out a Roth IRA if you're eligible. (4) Return to your 401(k) and increase contributions toward the annual limit. This order typically minimizes lifetime taxes while maximizing tax-advantaged growth.

It's Never Too Late to Start

While starting early produces the most dramatic results, starting later is still far better than not starting at all. Catch-up contribution provisions allow savers aged 50 and older to contribute meaningfully more to both IRAs and 401(k)s each year. A focused ten-year sprint in your 50s — maximizing contributions, eliminating debt, and keeping expenses low — can still produce a comfortable retirement.
