Credit Card Tips

Make the Most of Your Cards

Expert advice to help you maximize rewards, avoid fees, and use credit wisely.

Always Pay in Full

Carrying a balance erases your rewards. Pay your statement balance every month to avoid interest charges.

Match Card to Spending

Choose a card based on where you spend most — groceries, travel, dining, or gas — to maximize your earning rate.

Hit the Welcome Bonus

Sign-up bonuses are the biggest value you'll ever get from a card. Plan a large purchase to meet the spend requirement.

Track Your Annual Fee

Calculate your actual rewards each year. If benefits don't exceed the fee, downgrade or cancel before renewal.

Use Autopay

Set up autopay for at least the minimum payment to avoid late fees and protect your credit score.

Understand APR Tiers

Balance transfer cards often have separate (higher) purchase APRs. Read the fine print before carrying a balance.

Common Questions

Credit Card FAQs

How many credit cards should I have?
+
Most financial experts recommend 2–3 credit cards: one for everyday spending (like a flat-rate cash back card), one for a specific high-spend category (groceries or travel), and optionally one with no foreign transaction fees for travel. Having multiple cards can also help your credit utilization ratio, which benefits your score — as long as you pay them all in full each month.
Does applying for a credit card hurt your credit score?
+
Yes, briefly. A hard inquiry typically drops your score by 5–10 points and remains on your report for 2 years. However, the impact fades quickly — usually within a few months — especially if you're approved and start using the card responsibly. Avoid applying for multiple cards in a short window, as multiple inquiries compound the effect.
What credit score do I need for a top rewards card?
+
Most premium rewards cards (Chase Sapphire, Amex Gold, etc.) require a good to excellent credit score — typically 690 or above. Some require 740+. If your score is below 670, start with a secured card or a credit-builder card, use it for 12–18 months, then apply for a rewards card once your score improves.
Is a balance transfer card worth it?
+
Absolutely, if you have high-interest credit card debt. Moving a balance to a 0% APR card for 15–21 months can save hundreds or thousands in interest. The key is to pay off the full balance before the promotional period ends — and not add new charges to the card. Factor in the 3–5% balance transfer fee when calculating savings.
What's the difference between cash back and travel rewards?
+
Cash back cards give you a straightforward percentage of spending back as cash or statement credits — simple and flexible. Travel rewards cards earn points or miles redeemable for flights, hotels, and transfers to airline/hotel loyalty programs. Travel rewards can offer higher value per point (often 1.5–2¢ vs. 1¢ for cash back) but require more strategy to maximize.
