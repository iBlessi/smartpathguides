01 Buying Basics

New vs Used Cars: The Real Cost Comparison

The debate between buying new versus used is one of the biggest financial decisions a car buyer faces. In 2026, the average new car costs $49,388 while a three-year-old used vehicle averages $28,500 — a gap of nearly $21,000. But the sticker price only tells part of the story.

Depreciation is the hidden cost of new cars. A brand-new vehicle loses roughly 20% of its value the moment you drive it off the lot and approximately 60% over the first five years. That means a $50,000 new car is worth about $20,000 after five years — you've effectively "spent" $30,000 in depreciation alone. A used car that's already 2-3 years old has absorbed the steepest part of this curve, meaning your per-year depreciation cost drops significantly.

On the other hand, new cars come with advantages that have real dollar value: full manufacturer warranties (typically 3 years/36,000 miles bumper-to-bumper), the latest safety technology (automatic emergency braking, lane-keeping assist), lower interest rates on financing (often 1-3% less than used car loans), and occasionally manufacturer incentives like 0% APR promotions or cash-back offers worth $2,000-$5,000.

Certified Pre-Owned (CPO) vehicles offer a compelling middle ground. These are manufacturer-inspected used cars with extended warranties, often at prices 20-30% below their new equivalents. CPO programs from brands like Toyota, Lexus, Honda, and BMW are particularly strong, offering warranties that rival new-car coverage.

Here's a practical framework for deciding: if you plan to keep a car for 7+ years, buying new can make financial sense because you'll spread the depreciation cost over more years and benefit from warranty coverage during the priciest repair years. If you tend to swap cars every 3-4 years, buying used (or CPO) is almost always the smarter financial move. 02 Negotiation

How to Negotiate a Car Price Like a Pro

Most people dread negotiating at a dealership, but with the right preparation you can save $2,000 to $5,000 or more on your purchase. The key is understanding the dealer's cost structure and using information as your leverage.

Step 1: Know the Numbers Before You Arrive. Research the vehicle's invoice price (what the dealer paid), the MSRP (sticker price), and the fair market value on Kelley Blue Book and Edmunds. The dealer's profit margin on a new car is typically $1,500-$3,500 between invoice and MSRP. Your target should be $500-$1,000 above invoice price — dealers also receive manufacturer "holdback" payments of 2-3% that ensure they still profit.

Step 2: Get Multiple Quotes. Email the internet sales departments of 3-5 dealerships within a 50-mile radius with your exact specifications. Getting competing offers in writing gives you concrete numbers to negotiate against. Mention that you have other quotes without revealing the amounts — let dealers compete for your business.

Step 3: Negotiate the Purchase Price Separately. Dealers make money on four fronts: the vehicle price, your trade-in, financing, and add-ons. Negotiate each independently. If you lump them together, the dealer can shift numbers between categories to create the illusion of a deal. Start with the purchase price, then discuss trade-in, then financing last.

Step 4: Be Ready to Walk Away. This is genuinely the most powerful negotiation tool. If you're not getting the price you want, politely thank the salesperson and head for the door. More often than not, you'll get a call within 24 hours with a better offer. Dealerships spend significant money in advertising to get you through the door — they don't want to lose you.

Step 5: Watch for Add-On Traps in the Finance Office. After agreeing on a price, you'll move to the F&I (Finance and Insurance) office. This is where dealers make substantial profit on extended warranties, paint protection, fabric coating, nitrogen tire fills, and gap insurance. Most of these are either unnecessary or available cheaper elsewhere. The documentation fee is the one fee that's hardest to avoid, but it should be $100-$500 depending on your state.

03 Financing

Understanding Car Financing: Rates, Terms, and Hidden Fees

Car financing is where many buyers leave thousands of dollars on the table. Understanding how auto loans work — and where hidden costs lurk — is essential to getting the best deal.

Interest Rate (APR) is Everything. The Annual Percentage Rate determines how much you'll pay beyond the vehicle's price. In 2026, average new car rates range from 4.5% to 7.5% depending on your credit score. On a $35,000 loan over 60 months, the difference between a 5% and 8% rate is approximately $2,800 in total interest. That's real money that could go toward a better vehicle or your savings account.

Shorter Terms Save Serious Money. While 72-month and 84-month loans have become increasingly common (offering lower monthly payments), they cost dramatically more in total interest. A $30,000 loan at 6.5% over 48 months costs $4,139 in interest; the same loan over 84 months costs $7,538 — nearly double. You're also "underwater" (owing more than the car is worth) for a longer period, which is financially dangerous if the car is totaled or you need to sell.

Get Pre-Approved Before Visiting the Dealer. Walk into the dealership with a pre-approval letter from your bank or credit union. This serves two purposes: you know exactly what rate you qualify for, and you can use it as a negotiating tool if the dealer offers a higher rate. Credit unions typically offer rates 0.5-1.0% lower than traditional banks. Multiple loan applications within a 14-day window count as a single inquiry on your credit report, so shop around freely.

Hidden Fees to Watch For. Dealer markup on interest rates (a dealer who gets you approved at 5% may quote you 7% and pocket the difference), prepayment penalties (rare but still exist — confirm your loan has none), and loan packing (bundling products like extended warranties into the loan without clear disclosure). Always read every line of the contract before signing, and never sign paperwork with blank fields.

The 20/4/10 Rule is a solid guideline: put at least 20% down to avoid being underwater, finance for no more than 4 years (48 months) to limit interest costs, and keep your total monthly transportation costs (loan payment + insurance + fuel + maintenance) under 10% of your gross monthly income.

04 Leasing

Leasing vs Buying: Which Makes More Financial Sense?

Leasing versus buying is one of the most debated topics in car ownership. Each approach has genuine advantages, and the right choice depends on your driving habits, financial goals, and how long you keep vehicles.

How Leasing Works. When you lease, you're essentially paying for the vehicle's depreciation during your lease term (typically 24-36 months) plus interest (called the "money factor") and fees. At the end, you return the car or buy it at a predetermined residual value. Monthly lease payments are typically 30-40% lower than loan payments on the same vehicle because you're not paying for the full purchase price.

Buying Builds Equity. When you finance a purchase, every payment builds ownership equity. After your loan is paid off (typically 48-72 months), you own the car free and clear. Those payment-free years are where buying pulls ahead financially. A car bought for $40,000 might be worth $16,000 after 5 years — that's $16,000 in equity that a leaser never builds.

The True Cost Comparison. Over a 6-year period, leasing two 3-year vehicles typically costs 15-25% more than buying one car and keeping it for the full 6 years. However, the leaser always drives a newer car with full warranty coverage, the latest safety tech, and lower maintenance costs. Over a 10-year period, buying wins decisively because you get 4-5 years of no-payment driving.

Leasing Makes Sense When: You drive fewer than 12,000-15,000 miles per year (mileage penalties can be steep at $0.15-$0.25/mile), you want a new car every 2-3 years, you use the car for business (lease payments are often tax-deductible), or you prefer predictable monthly costs without surprise repair bills. Buying Makes Sense When: You plan to keep cars for 5+ years, you drive more than 15,000 miles annually, you want to modify your vehicle, or you want to eventually be payment-free.

05 Inspection

The Complete Pre-Purchase Inspection Checklist

Never buy a used car without a thorough inspection. A $100-200 pre-purchase inspection (PPI) from an independent mechanic can save you thousands in unexpected repairs. Here's what to check yourself before investing in a professional inspection.

Exterior Check: Walk around the vehicle looking for uneven panel gaps (indicating accident repair), mismatched paint or overspray on rubber seals, rust around wheel wells and door bottoms, cracked or foggy headlights, and tire wear patterns (uneven wear indicates alignment or suspension problems). Run your hand along body panels — ripples or bumps under the paint suggest filler from body work.

Under the Hood: Check the oil dipstick — the oil should be amber to dark brown and free of metal flakes or milky residue (which indicates a head gasket leak). Inspect belts and hoses for cracks or fraying. Look for corrosion on the battery terminals. Check all fluid levels and colors: transmission fluid should be red or pink (brown means it needs service), coolant should be bright green or orange (rust-colored means trouble).

Interior Inspection: Test every electronic feature: windows, locks, mirrors, A/C (both heat and cold), radio, Bluetooth, navigation, seat adjustments, and sunroof. Sniff for musty odors (flood damage) or overpowering air freshener (hiding something). Check the carpet under floor mats for water stains. Inspect the headliner, seats, and dashboard for excessive wear that doesn't match the stated mileage.

Test Drive Essentials: Drive for at least 20-30 minutes on highways, city streets, and rough roads. Listen for unusual sounds during acceleration, braking, and turning. Test the brakes — the car should stop straight without pulling or vibration. Check the transmission for smooth shifting (automatic) or easy gear engagement (manual). Note how the car handles bumps and potholes — excessive bouncing means worn shocks.

Documentation Deep Dive: Verify the VIN matches on the title, dashboard, and door jamb. Run a Carfax or AutoCheck report. Review available service records — consistent maintenance history is a strong indicator of a well-cared-for vehicle. Check for open recalls at NHTSA.gov. Confirm the title is clean (not salvage, rebuilt, or flood). Finally, have a trusted mechanic perform a full PPI including a compression test and diagnostic scan for stored error codes.

06 Powertrains

Electric vs Hybrid vs Gas: Which Powertrain Is Right for You?

The automotive landscape in 2026 offers three distinct powertrain choices, each with significant implications for your wallet, lifestyle, and environmental impact. Here's an honest comparison to help you decide.

Gasoline Vehicles remain the most affordable upfront, with the widest selection and the most extensive refueling infrastructure. Average fuel cost for a gas vehicle driving 12,000 miles per year is approximately $1,800-$2,400 annually. Maintenance costs are moderate — expect oil changes ($40-80 every 5,000-7,500 miles), brake replacements, and eventual transmission or engine work at higher mileages. Gas cars make the most sense if you frequently drive long distances, live in an area with limited charging infrastructure, or prioritize low purchase price.

Hybrid Vehicles (HEV and PHEV) combine a gas engine with an electric motor. Standard hybrids (like the Toyota Prius) self-charge through regenerative braking and offer 45-60 MPG with no plugging in required. Plug-in hybrids (PHEVs) have larger batteries offering 25-50 miles of electric-only range before switching to gas — perfect for short commutes on electric with gas backup for longer trips. Hybrids cost $2,000-$5,000 more than equivalent gas models but save $600-$1,000 per year in fuel. They're ideal for drivers who want fuel savings without range anxiety.

Electric Vehicles (EVs) have the highest upfront cost (averaging $5,000-$12,000 more than gas equivalents) but the lowest operating costs. Electricity costs roughly $500-$800 per year for 12,000 miles of driving versus $1,800-$2,400 for gas. Maintenance is dramatically lower — no oil changes, fewer brake replacements (regenerative braking reduces wear), and no transmission service. Federal tax credits up to $7,500 (and additional state incentives) can significantly close the purchase price gap.

The Breakeven Calculation: If an EV costs $8,000 more than its gas equivalent, and you save $1,500/year in fuel and $400/year in maintenance, the EV breaks even in approximately 4.2 years. After the tax credit, breakeven can happen in under 1 year. The longer you plan to keep the car, the more EVs make financial sense. However, consider your charging situation — if you can't charge at home, public charging costs more and adds inconvenience.

07 Knowledge

How to Read a Window Sticker (Monroney Label) Explained

Every new car in America is required by federal law to display a window sticker (officially called the Monroney label, after Senator Mike Monroney who championed the Automobile Information Disclosure Act of 1958). This document contains critical pricing and specification information that many buyers overlook.

MSRP (Manufacturer's Suggested Retail Price) is prominently displayed at the bottom. This is the base price of the vehicle plus all factory-installed options and the destination/delivery charge. Remember: MSRP is a suggestion, not a requirement. In a buyer's market, you should pay below MSRP. In tight supply situations (as with some popular EVs and trucks), dealers may add a "market adjustment" above MSRP — walk away from significant markups unless the vehicle is extremely rare.

Standard Equipment vs. Optional Equipment. The sticker itemizes what comes standard on that trim level and what optional packages were added. Each option's individual price is listed. Use this to verify you're getting what you're paying for and to identify options you might not want. A $48,000 sticker price might include $6,000 in optional packages you don't need — a lower trim level could save significant money with only minor feature sacrifices.

Fuel Economy Ratings from the EPA are displayed as city, highway, and combined MPG (or MPGe for EVs). These are estimates based on standardized testing — real-world fuel economy typically comes in 5-15% lower. Use these numbers for comparing vehicles against each other rather than as absolute predictions of your fuel costs. The estimated annual fuel cost and fuel economy comparison to similar vehicles are also shown.

Safety Ratings from NHTSA (if available at time of manufacture) appear as a star rating system. Five stars is the highest rating. Look for both overall and category-specific ratings (frontal crash, side crash, rollover). Some stickers also reference IIHS safety ratings, which use a different "Good/Acceptable/Marginal/Poor" scale. Prioritize vehicles with top ratings in both testing systems.

Destination Charge is the non-negotiable fee for shipping the vehicle from the factory to the dealer, typically $995-$1,695. While you can't negotiate this fee (it's set by the manufacturer), you should ensure it's included in MSRP quotes so dealers don't try to add it separately. The VIN, production date, and place of manufacture are also listed on the sticker for your records.

08 Insurance

Car Insurance 101: Coverage Types and How to Save

Car insurance is a mandatory expense that can cost $1,500-$3,000+ per year depending on your profile and coverage choices. Understanding the different coverage types helps you buy the right protection without overpaying.

Liability Coverage (Required) pays for damage you cause to others. It's expressed as three numbers like 100/300/100 — $100K per person for bodily injury, $300K total per accident for bodily injury, and $100K for property damage. Most financial advisors recommend at least 100/300/100. Minimum state requirements (often 25/50/25) leave you personally exposed if you cause a serious accident.

Collision Coverage pays to repair your car after an accident regardless of fault. Comprehensive Coverage covers non-accident damage — theft, vandalism, hail, flooding, animal strikes, and falling objects. Both are optional but required if you have a car loan or lease. Consider dropping these when your car's value drops below $5,000-$8,000, as the premiums may exceed potential payouts.

Uninsured/Underinsured Motorist Coverage protects you when the at-fault driver has no insurance or insufficient coverage. Approximately 14% of drivers are uninsured nationally, with rates above 20% in some states. This coverage is inexpensive and highly recommended. Medical Payments / Personal Injury Protection (PIP) covers medical expenses for you and your passengers regardless of fault — essential if you don't have robust health insurance.

How to Save on Car Insurance: Shop around annually — loyalty rarely pays in insurance. Get quotes from at least 5 providers including a direct writer (GEICO, Progressive), an independent agent, and a regional insurer. Bundle with home or renter's insurance for 10-25% discounts. Increase your deductible from $500 to $1,000 to save 15-25% on premiums. Ask about low-mileage discounts if you drive under 7,500 miles annually. Maintain a clean driving record — one accident can raise rates 30-50% for 3-5 years.

Discounts to Always Ask About: Multi-policy bundle, good driver, good student (under 25), defensive driving course, pay-in-full, paperless billing, vehicle safety features, anti-theft devices, and professional/alumni organization affiliations. Many insurers also now offer usage-based programs where your rate reflects your actual driving behavior — beneficial if you're a cautious, low-mileage driver.

09 Trade-Ins

Trade-In Tips: How to Maximize Your Old Car's Value

Your trade-in is one of the biggest negotiating variables in a car deal. The average trade-in is worth $8,000-$15,000, and the difference between a lowball offer and fair market value can easily be $2,000-$4,000. Here's how to get top dollar.

Know Your Car's Worth Before the Dealer Does. Check values on Kelley Blue Book (kbb.com), Edmunds, and NADA Guides. Get the trade-in value, private party value, and dealer retail value. Also get instant cash offers from Carvana, CarMax, and Vroom — these serve as concrete floor prices. Walk into the dealership knowing the fair range, and don't accept anything significantly below the KBB fair trade-in value.

Cosmetic Preparation Pays Off. A clean car legitimately appraises for $500-$1,500 more than a dirty one. Before your appraisal: wash and wax the exterior, vacuum and shampoo the interior, clean all glass, treat leather or vinyl surfaces, and remove personal items and clutter. A $200 professional detail can yield a 5-10x return on investment. Fix minor cosmetic issues like burned-out bulbs, cracked windshield (if insurance covers it), and small dents (paintless dent repair costs $75-150 per dent).

Mechanical Preparation. Have maintenance current — an oil change receipt from last month signals a cared-for vehicle. Make sure tires have adequate tread (a failed tire check kills trade-in value). Fix any check engine lights if the repair is under $300 — an illuminated warning light can reduce your offer by $1,000 or more. Gather all service records, owner's manuals, and spare keys — a second key fob alone can be worth $200-$400.

Timing and Strategy. Get your trade-in appraised at 2-3 dealerships and CarMax before negotiating your new purchase. Present the highest offer as your starting point. Remember to negotiate the trade-in value separately from the new car price — never let the dealer combine the numbers. If you're buying a new car, trade-ins offer a tax advantage in most states: you only pay sales tax on the difference between the new car price and your trade-in value, potentially saving hundreds or thousands in taxes.

When to Skip the Trade-In: If your vehicle is worth over $15,000 and is in good condition, consider selling privately. Private sales typically yield 10-20% more than trade-in value. The trade-off is time, effort, and dealing with potential buyers. For vehicles worth under $5,000 or those needing significant repairs, a trade-in is usually the better option for convenience.

10 Timing

Best Time of Year to Buy a Car and Why It Matters

Timing your car purchase strategically can save $1,000 to $5,000 — sometimes more. Dealers operate on monthly, quarterly, and annual sales cycles, and understanding these rhythms gives you an edge.

End of Year (December): This is widely considered the best time to buy. Dealers are pushing to hit annual sales targets (which trigger manufacturer bonuses), clearing out current-year inventory to make room for next year's models, and offering year-end sales events. Combine this with holiday promotions, and December consistently delivers the deepest discounts — averaging 6-8% below MSRP on many models.

End of Model Year (August-October): When new model year vehicles arrive, the previous year's models become "last year's inventory" even though they're functionally brand-new. Dealers discount these vehicles aggressively to clear floor space. You can often save $3,000-$7,000 on an outgoing model with identical features — the only "downside" is a model year that's one year older on the title, which has minimal impact on resale value.

End of Month/Quarter: Salespeople and dealerships have monthly quotas. The last 2-3 days of any month — and especially the last days of March, June, September, and December (quarter-ends) — create urgency to close deals. Salespeople may accept slimmer profits to hit their bonus thresholds. This doesn't guarantee a great deal, but it increases your negotiating leverage significantly.

Holiday Weekends: Presidents' Day, Memorial Day, Fourth of July, Labor Day, and Black Friday are major car-selling events with manufacturer-sponsored incentives (rebates, special APR, bonus cash). These promotions are real and can stack with other discounts. However, dealerships are also crowded, so you'll get less personalized attention. Consider researching and negotiating via email before showing up.

Best Day of the Week: Monday and Tuesday are typically the slowest days at dealerships. Salespeople have more time to work with you, the finance office is less backed up, and there's more motivation to make a deal when the lot is quiet. Avoid Saturdays when possible — they're the busiest day, and dealerships have less incentive to negotiate when they're juggling multiple customers.

When NOT to Buy: Spring (especially March-May) is peak buying season with higher prices and less negotiating room. The first month after a popular model launches typically features markups rather than discounts. If you're in no rush, patience is the best negotiating tool — waiting for the right timing can save more than any negotiation tactic.

Auto Buying Glossary

Key terms every car buyer should know. Bookmark this page for quick reference during your car buying journey.

APR (Annual Percentage Rate)

The yearly cost of borrowing money, expressed as a percentage. Includes the interest rate plus certain fees. Lower APR means lower total cost of your loan.

MSRP

Manufacturer's Suggested Retail Price — the "sticker price" set by the automaker. This is a starting point for negotiation, not a fixed price.

Invoice Price

The price the dealer paid the manufacturer for the vehicle. Typically 5-10% below MSRP. A useful benchmark for negotiating a fair deal.

Residual Value

The projected value of a vehicle at the end of a lease term. Higher residual value means lower monthly lease payments. Expressed as a percentage of MSRP.

Gap Insurance

Guaranteed Asset Protection insurance. Covers the difference between what you owe on your loan and the car's actual value if it's totaled or stolen.

Lemon Law

State laws that protect buyers who purchase vehicles with substantial manufacturing defects that can't be fixed after a reasonable number of repair attempts.

VIN (Vehicle Identification Number)

A unique 17-character code assigned to every vehicle. Used to track recalls, registrations, warranty claims, and vehicle history reports.

Odometer Disclosure

A legal requirement for sellers to disclose the vehicle's accurate mileage at the time of sale. Odometer fraud (rollback) is a federal crime.

Title

The legal document proving vehicle ownership. Shows the owner's name, VIN, and any liens. A "clean title" means no major damage history or outstanding debts.

Lien

A legal claim a lender has on your vehicle until the loan is paid off. The lienholder is listed on the title. The lien is released when the loan is satisfied.

Depreciation

The loss in value of a vehicle over time. New cars lose ~20% in the first year and ~60% over 5 years. The single largest cost of car ownership.

Money Factor

The interest rate used in lease calculations. Multiply by 2,400 to convert to an approximate APR. A money factor of 0.0025 equals roughly 6% APR.

Capitalized Cost (Cap Cost)

The negotiated price of a leased vehicle — essentially the selling price used to calculate your lease payment. Lower cap cost means lower payments.

Destination Charge

The fee for transporting a new vehicle from the factory to the dealer. Typically $995-$1,695. Non-negotiable and set by the manufacturer.

Doc Fee (Documentation Fee)

A dealer charge for processing paperwork. Ranges from $100-$700+ depending on state regulations. Some states cap this fee; in others, it's negotiable.

CPO (Certified Pre-Owned)

A used vehicle that has passed a manufacturer-specified inspection and comes with an extended warranty. Typically costs more than a regular used car but less than new.

Holdback

A percentage (typically 2-3%) of MSRP that the manufacturer pays back to the dealer after the vehicle is sold. This is hidden profit that provides negotiation room.

Underwater / Upside Down

When you owe more on your car loan than the vehicle is worth. Common with long loan terms and small down payments. Makes selling or trading in difficult.

Trade-In Value

The amount a dealer will credit you for your existing vehicle toward a new purchase. Typically 10-20% less than private sale value but more convenient.

Private Party Value

The price you could expect to receive selling your vehicle directly to another individual, without a dealer as intermediary. Usually higher than trade-in value.

F&I (Finance & Insurance)

The dealership department where you finalize financing, sign paperwork, and are offered add-on products like extended warranties and protection plans.

Extended Warranty

A service contract that covers repairs beyond the manufacturer's original warranty period. Can be valuable for certain vehicles but often overpriced at the dealer.

Monroney Label

The official name for the window sticker required on all new vehicles sold in the US. Lists MSRP, standard/optional equipment, fuel economy, and safety ratings.

Pre-Approval

A conditional loan commitment from a lender based on your credit and income. Gives you a rate and loan amount before visiting the dealer. Strengthens your negotiating position.

Rebate / Cash Back

A manufacturer incentive that reduces the effective purchase price. Can sometimes be combined with special financing, but often you must choose one or the other.

Lease Disposition Fee

A fee charged at lease end (typically $300-$500) if you return the vehicle instead of purchasing it. Some lessors waive this if you lease another vehicle from them.

Acquisition Fee

A fee charged by the leasing company to originate the lease, typically $595-$995. Usually non-negotiable and rolled into the lease payment.

Mileage Penalty

The charge for exceeding your lease's annual mileage allowance (usually 10,000-15,000 miles/year). Typically $0.15-$0.25 per excess mile. Can add up quickly.

Salvage Title

Indicates a vehicle was declared a total loss by an insurance company due to damage, theft, or flood. Significantly reduces value and may affect insurability.

Rebuilt Title

A salvage vehicle that has been repaired and inspected to meet state safety standards. Still carries a stigma that reduces resale value by 20-40%.

Dealer Prep Fee

A charge for "preparing" the vehicle for sale (washing, removing plastic, etc.). This is a profit center — the manufacturer already reimburses the dealer for this. Decline or negotiate it.

Market Adjustment / ADM

Additional Dealer Markup — an amount added above MSRP on high-demand vehicles. Not set by the manufacturer. Negotiate or find a dealer selling at MSRP.

Buyout Price

The amount you can pay to purchase your leased vehicle at lease end. Equals the residual value plus applicable taxes and fees. Compare to market value before deciding.

Prepayment Penalty

A fee some lenders charge for paying off your loan early. Rare in auto loans but always confirm before signing. Most modern auto loans have no prepayment penalty.

NHTSA

National Highway Traffic Safety Administration — the federal agency responsible for vehicle safety standards, crash testing, and recalls. Check NHTSA.gov for open recalls on any vehicle.

Powertrain Warranty

Covers the engine, transmission, and drivetrain components. Typically 5 years/60,000 miles for most brands. Hyundai and Kia offer 10 years/100,000 miles.

Frequently Asked Questions

Detailed answers to the most common car buying questions.

What credit score do I need to buy a car?
While you can get a car loan with a credit score as low as 500, scores of 670+ qualify for the best rates. A score above 740 is considered excellent and will secure the lowest APR available, typically 3-5% for new cars in 2026. Subprime borrowers (below 580) may face rates of 10-20% or higher. Before applying, check your credit report at AnnualCreditReport.com for free and dispute any errors that could be dragging your score down.
How much should I spend on a car?
Financial experts recommend the 20/4/10 rule: put at least 20% down, finance for no more than 4 years, and keep total transportation costs (payment, insurance, fuel, maintenance) under 10% of your gross monthly income. For example, on a $60,000 salary, aim for total car costs under $500/month. Stretching beyond this guideline is one of the most common financial mistakes car buyers make.
Is it better to buy new or used?
Used cars typically offer better value since new cars depreciate 20-30% in the first year alone. A 2-3 year old certified pre-owned vehicle offers the best balance of value and reliability. However, new cars offer the latest safety features, full warranties, current financing incentives, and the peace of mind that comes with knowing the complete vehicle history. If you plan to keep a car for 7+ years, buying new can be cost-competitive.
Should I get pre-approved for a car loan before going to the dealer?
Absolutely. Getting pre-approved from your bank or credit union before visiting the dealership gives you leverage in negotiations, a clear understanding of your budget, and a baseline interest rate to compare against the dealer's financing offer. Multiple loan inquiries within a 14-day window count as a single credit check, so shop around freely. Walk in with your pre-approval letter and let the dealer try to beat it.
What is the best time of year to buy a car?
The best times to buy are end of December (year-end clearance), end of model year (August-October when new models arrive), holiday weekends (Memorial Day, Labor Day, Black Friday), and end of any month or quarter when salespeople push to hit quotas. Monday and Tuesday visits tend to get more personal attention from salespeople. Avoid spring months when demand peaks and dealers have less incentive to negotiate.
How do I negotiate a lower car price?
Research the invoice price and fair market value on sites like KBB and Edmunds. Get quotes from multiple dealers via email. Negotiate the purchase price separately from your trade-in and financing. Be willing to walk away — this is your strongest tool. Start your offer 5-10% below the fair market value and work upward. Focus on the total out-the-door price, not just the monthly payment.
What is gap insurance and do I need it?
Gap insurance covers the difference between what you owe on your car loan and the car's actual cash value if it's totaled or stolen. You need it if your down payment was less than 20%, your loan term is longer than 60 months, or you're leasing (often required by the leasing company). Buy it from your auto insurance company, not the dealer — it's typically 2-3x more expensive at the dealership. Expect to pay $20-40/year through your insurer versus $400-800 at the dealer.
Is leasing or buying a car better?
Buying is generally better financially over the long term since you build equity and eventually own the car outright. Leasing offers lower monthly payments and a new car every 2-3 years, but you never build equity and face mileage restrictions. Leasing makes sense if you drive fewer than 12,000 miles annually, want the latest features, and prefer predictable costs. Buying makes sense if you keep cars for 5+ years and want payment-free years after the loan ends. Use our Lease vs Buy calculator for a personalized comparison.
What should I look for during a test drive?
Test drive for at least 30 minutes including highway and city driving. Check acceleration, braking, steering feel, blind spots, road noise, and comfort. Test technology features like Bluetooth, navigation, and parking assist. Drive over bumps to check suspension. Sit in the back seat to check rear passenger comfort. Try parking to assess visibility and turning radius. If buying used, listen for unusual sounds and note how the transmission shifts — hesitation or clunking indicates potential problems.
How much does car insurance cost for a new car?
Average full-coverage car insurance in 2026 costs approximately $1,800-$2,400 per year, depending on your age, location, driving record, and vehicle type. SUVs and trucks tend to cost more to insure than sedans. EVs can cost 15-25% more due to higher repair costs. Always shop at least 3-5 quotes before committing, and consider bundling with home or renter's insurance for discounts of 10-25%.
What is a good interest rate for a car loan in 2026?
In 2026, good interest rates for new cars range from 4.5-6.5% for borrowers with good credit (670+), while used car rates typically run 5.5-8.5%. Excellent credit (740+) can secure rates under 4.5% on new vehicles. Credit unions often offer rates 0.5-1% lower than traditional banks. Always compare at least 3 lender offers, and remember that manufacturer promotions sometimes offer 0% or very low rates on select models.
Should I trade in my car or sell it privately?
Selling privately typically gets you 10-20% more than a trade-in, but requires more effort including advertising, meeting buyers, and handling paperwork. Trade-ins are faster, offer potential tax savings in most states (you only pay sales tax on the price difference), and eliminate the hassle. For vehicles worth over $15,000, the extra effort of selling privately is usually worthwhile. Get a trade-in appraisal first as a baseline, then decide if the potential private sale premium justifies the extra work.
What are dealer fees I should watch out for?
Common fees to scrutinize: documentation fee ($100-700, negotiable in most states), dealer prep fee (should be free — the manufacturer already pays for this), nitrogen tire fill ($0 real value), fabric protection/paint sealant ($50-100 value charged at $500+), extended warranties (often significantly overpriced at the dealer), and VIN etching ($20 DIY kit vs $300+ at dealer). Always request an itemized bill and question every line before signing.
How do I check a used car's history?
Run a vehicle history report through Carfax or AutoCheck using the VIN (Vehicle Identification Number) found on the dashboard (driver's side, visible through windshield) and the driver's door jamb. The report reveals accidents, title status (salvage, flood, rebuilt), number of previous owners, service records, and odometer readings. Also check for open recalls at NHTSA.gov. For the most thorough evaluation, invest $100-200 in a pre-purchase inspection by an independent mechanic.
Are electric vehicles worth it in 2026?
EVs make strong financial sense in 2026 if you drive 12,000+ miles per year and can charge at home. Fuel savings average $800-1,500/year compared to gas vehicles, maintenance costs are roughly 40% lower (no oil changes, fewer brake replacements due to regenerative braking), and federal tax credits of up to $7,500 significantly reduce the purchase price. The main considerations are the higher upfront cost (still $5-10K more than comparable gas models), charging infrastructure in your area, and whether the typical 200-350 mile range meets your daily driving needs.
