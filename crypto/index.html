Frequently Asked Questions

Answers to the most common questions about cryptocurrency in 2026.

What is Bitcoin and how does it work? +

Bitcoin (BTC) is a decentralized digital currency created in 2009 by the pseudonymous Satoshi Nakamoto. Unlike the US dollar or euro, Bitcoin is not issued or controlled by any government or central bank. Instead, it runs on a global peer-to-peer network secured by cryptography.

At its core, Bitcoin uses a technology called the blockchain — a public, tamper-resistant ledger that permanently records every transaction ever made. When you send Bitcoin, the transfer is broadcast to thousands of computers (nodes) worldwide. Specialized participants called "miners" compete to bundle these transactions into blocks and add them to the chain, earning newly created Bitcoin as a reward.

Bitcoin has a fixed maximum supply of 21 million coins, a deliberate design choice that creates digital scarcity similar to gold. Roughly 19.7 million BTC have already been mined. This hard cap is one reason many investors view Bitcoin as a long-term store of value and an inflation hedge.

What's the difference between Bitcoin and Ethereum? +

Bitcoin and Ethereum are both leading cryptocurrencies, but they serve fundamentally different purposes. Bitcoin was designed primarily as a store of value and medium of exchange — digital money. Ethereum, launched in 2015, was built as a programmable blockchain platform.

The key difference is smart contracts: self-executing code that lives on Ethereum's blockchain and automatically enforces agreement terms without intermediaries. This enables an entire ecosystem of decentralized applications (dApps), including DeFi protocols, NFT marketplaces, and gaming platforms.

From a technical standpoint, Ethereum uses a "proof-of-stake" consensus mechanism (switched from proof-of-work in 2022), which is far more energy efficient than Bitcoin's mining. As of early 2026, more than 33 million ETH tokens are staked to secure the network. Bitcoin, by contrast, still relies on energy-intensive mining. Think of Bitcoin as digital gold and Ethereum as a global programmable computer.

How do I buy cryptocurrency safely? +

The safest way to buy cryptocurrency is through a reputable, regulated centralized exchange such as Coinbase, Kraken, or Gemini. These platforms verify your identity (KYC), comply with financial regulations, and are required to report transactions to the IRS starting with the 2025 tax year via the new Form 1099-DA.

Here's a safe buying process: (1) Create an account on a regulated exchange and complete identity verification. (2) Enable two-factor authentication (use an authenticator app, not SMS). (3) Link a bank account or debit card. (4) Start small — invest only what you can afford to lose. (5) Transfer significant holdings off the exchange to a personal wallet.

Always bookmark the official URL of any exchange you use — phishing sites that mimic legitimate platforms are among the most common crypto scams in 2026. Never click on sponsored search ads to access an exchange; type the URL directly or use your saved bookmark.

What is a crypto wallet and which type is best? +

A crypto wallet doesn't actually "store" your cryptocurrency — it stores your private keys, the cryptographic credentials that prove ownership and authorize transactions on the blockchain. Lose your private keys, and you lose access to your funds permanently.

There are two main categories: hot wallets (connected to the internet) and cold wallets (offline). Hot wallets include exchange accounts, browser extensions like MetaMask, and mobile apps — they're convenient for frequent trading but more vulnerable to hacks. Cold wallets are hardware devices like Ledger or Trezor that store keys offline, making them immune to remote attacks.

Best practice: use a hardware wallet for the bulk of your holdings (anything you don't plan to trade soon) and keep only what you actively use in a hot wallet. Always write down your 12–24 word seed phrase and store it offline in a secure physical location. Never photograph it or store it digitally. The seed phrase is the only way to recover your wallet if your device is lost or damaged.

What are gas fees? +

Gas fees are transaction fees paid to the validators who process and confirm transactions on a blockchain network. The term "gas" originally comes from Ethereum, where it measures the computational effort required to execute an operation. Just as a car needs gasoline to run, every Ethereum transaction requires a certain amount of gas to be processed.

Gas fees fluctuate based on network congestion — when many people are transacting simultaneously, fees spike. In 2026, Ethereum's Layer 2 networks (like Base, Arbitrum, and Optimism) have dramatically reduced this problem, bringing average fees down to around $0.15 or less for many transactions, compared to several dollars on the Ethereum mainnet during peak periods.

Practical tips: check current gas prices at etherscan.io before transacting; schedule non-urgent transactions during off-peak hours (typically weekday mornings US time); and consider using Layer 2 solutions for frequent, smaller transactions. Gas fees on Bitcoin work similarly but are called "transaction fees" and are measured in satoshis per byte of data.

Is cryptocurrency taxable in 2026? +

Yes — the IRS classifies cryptocurrency as property, meaning every disposal is a taxable event. This includes selling crypto for cash, swapping one token for another, and using crypto to buy goods or services. Receiving crypto as income — from staking, mining, airdrops, or employment — is also taxable as ordinary income at its fair market value when received.

Two major changes took effect for the 2025 tax year (filed in 2026): Form 1099-DA is now required from all covered digital asset brokers, including major centralized exchanges like Coinbase, meaning the IRS receives a copy of your trading activity automatically. Additionally, cost basis must now be tracked on a per-wallet or per-account basis — you can no longer pool all holdings of the same coin across different platforms.

Gains on crypto held more than one year qualify for the lower long-term capital gains tax rate (0%, 15%, or 20% depending on income). Short-term gains are taxed as ordinary income. Use crypto tax software like CoinTracker or Koinly to automatically track cost basis across wallets, and consider consulting a tax professional familiar with digital assets for complex portfolios.

What is DeFi (decentralized finance)? +

DeFi, or decentralized finance, is a financial system built on public blockchains (primarily Ethereum) that replicates traditional banking services — lending, borrowing, trading, earning interest — without relying on banks or brokers as intermediaries. Instead of a loan officer approving your application, a piece of open-source code called a smart contract automatically enforces the rules.

By Q3 2025, DeFi reached a milestone of $237 billion in total value locked across protocols. You can deposit cryptocurrency and earn yield, borrow against your holdings as collateral, trade tokens directly from your wallet on a decentralized exchange (DEX), or contribute to liquidity pools to earn a share of trading fees — typically 3–8% APY.

The tradeoffs are real: DeFi is open 24/7 and accessible globally, but it carries significant risks. Smart contract bugs can be exploited, resulting in permanent loss of funds with no customer support or FDIC insurance to rely on. Start with small amounts and well-audited protocols like Uniswap or Aave, and never invest more in DeFi than you are prepared to lose entirely.

What are NFTs and are they still relevant in 2026? +

NFTs (non-fungible tokens) are unique cryptographic tokens on a blockchain that represent ownership of a specific item — originally digital art, but now much broader. Unlike Bitcoin, where every coin is identical (fungible), each NFT is one-of-a-kind and can represent art, music, in-game assets, event tickets, membership credentials, or even real-world property records.

The speculative art-NFT bubble of 2021–2022 has largely deflated — total NFT market cap dropped from roughly $9 billion in 2024 to about $2.4 billion by early 2026, and major Web2 companies like Reddit and Nike exited the space. However, NFTs are far from dead. The technology is evolving toward utility-driven applications: gaming NFTs now represent 38% of total Ethereum NFT transaction volume, and new use cases include tokenized event tickets, DAO voting rights, and AI agent identities on-chain.

If you are considering NFTs in 2026, focus on projects with clear, real-world utility rather than speculation. Approach the market with realistic expectations, since the days of 10x flipping digital pictures are largely over. The underlying technology remains powerful; it's the speculative "get rich quick" use case that lost relevance.

How do I spot and avoid crypto scams? +

Crypto scams cost victims $9.3 billion in 2024 alone — a 66% increase from the year before — and are growing more sophisticated with AI deepfakes in 2026. Knowing the most common tactics is your best defense.

Red flags to watch for: Promises of "guaranteed" or risk-free returns (no legitimate investment offers this). Requests for your private key or seed phrase (no real platform ever asks). Urgency pressure ("this offer expires in one hour"). Unsolicited messages on Telegram, Discord, or social media offering investment tips. AI-generated deepfake videos of celebrity endorsements. Typosquatted URLs one letter off from real exchanges (e.g., "coimbase.com").

Common scam types in 2026: Pig butchering (romance scams that slowly build trust before directing you to a fake platform), rug pulls (developers abandoning a project after attracting investment), fake wallet apps, and "recovery agent" scams that target people who have already been defrauded. If you believe you've been scammed, report it to the FTC at ReportFraud.ftc.gov and the FBI's IC3 at ic3.gov.

What is staking and how does it earn passive income? +

Staking is the process of locking up cryptocurrency in a proof-of-stake blockchain to help validate transactions and secure the network. In return, stakers receive newly issued tokens and a share of transaction fees — essentially earning interest on crypto you already own without actively trading it.

By early 2026, staking has gone mainstream. More than 33 million ETH (worth roughly $100 billion) is staked on Ethereum alone. Major proof-of-stake networks — including Ethereum (ETH), Cardano (ADA), Solana (SOL), and Polkadot (DOT) — typically offer annual staking rewards of 4–15% APY, though rates vary by network conditions and the amount staked. Staking on platforms like Coinbase or Lido allows participation without the technical complexity of running a validator node.

Key risks to understand: Staking rewards are taxable as ordinary income in the US. Some platforms impose "lock-up" periods during which you cannot sell your staked tokens — if the price drops significantly during that window, losses can exceed your earned rewards. Choose established networks with long track records, and only stake with reputable, audited platforms to minimize counterparty risk.
