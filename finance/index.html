FAQ

Financial Questions Answered

How much house can I afford?

A general rule is that your monthly mortgage payment should not exceed 28% of your gross monthly income (the "front-end ratio"). Including all debt payments, keep total debt below 36% (the "back-end ratio"). Use our mortgage calculator above to determine your specific monthly payment. Remember to factor in property taxes (typically 1-2% of home value annually), homeowner's insurance (~$1,200/year on average), and HOA fees if applicable.

How much should I save for retirement?

Financial advisors typically recommend saving 10-15% of your gross income for retirement, including any employer match. Common benchmarks: by age 30, have 1x your annual salary saved; by 40, 3x; by 50, 6x; by 60, 8x; by 67, 10x. If you're behind, maximize contributions to tax-advantaged accounts (401k: $23,500 limit in 2026; IRA: $7,000 limit). The most important factor is starting early — time and compounding do the heavy lifting.

What is compound interest and why does it matter?

Compound interest is interest earned on both your initial principal and accumulated interest. $10,000 at 7% annual return becomes $19,672 after 10 years, $38,697 after 20 years, and $76,123 after 30 years — without adding a single dollar. The key insight: time is your most powerful variable. Starting 10 years earlier can double your final wealth even with identical contributions.

How much emergency fund do I need?

Most financial experts recommend 3-6 months of essential living expenses in an accessible account. If you have variable income, are self-employed, have dependents, or work in a volatile industry, aim for 6-12 months. Keep emergency funds in a high-yield savings account (currently paying 4-5% APY) rather than a checking account. Use our Emergency Fund Calculator above to find your specific target.

Should I pay off debt or invest?

Follow this priority order: (1) Contribute enough to your 401k to get the full employer match — it's a guaranteed 50-100% return. (2) Build a 3-month emergency fund. (3) Pay off high-interest debt (credit cards, personal loans above 8-10%). (4) Max out Roth IRA. (5) Continue investing. If debt APR is below ~7%, you could reasonably invest instead, but eliminating debt provides a guaranteed return equal to the interest rate.

What is the 50/30/20 budget rule?

Allocate after-tax income: 50% for needs (housing, food, insurance, minimum debt payments), 30% for wants (dining out, entertainment, subscriptions, travel), 20% for savings and extra debt payments. It's a flexible framework — not a rigid rule. In high cost-of-living areas, housing alone may consume 35-40%, requiring adjustments in other categories. The goal is intentional allocation, not hitting exact percentages.

How is my credit score calculated?

FICO scores (300-850) use five factors: payment history 35% (most important — never miss a payment), amounts owed/utilization 30% (keep credit card balances below 30% of limit, ideally below 10%), length of credit history 15% (don't close old accounts), new credit inquiries 10% (multiple applications in a short window hurt), credit mix 10% (having both revolving and installment accounts helps). A score above 740 typically qualifies for the best rates.

What is the difference between APR and interest rate?

The interest rate is the cost of borrowing the principal amount only. The APR (Annual Percentage Rate) includes the interest rate plus other loan costs — origination fees, closing costs, mortgage points, and mortgage insurance — expressed as a yearly rate. APR gives a more complete picture of total borrowing cost. When comparing loans, compare APRs, not just interest rates. For mortgages, the difference between interest rate and APR can be 0.3-0.5%.

How do I calculate my net worth?

Net Worth = Total Assets − Total Liabilities. Assets: cash, checking/savings accounts, investment accounts, retirement accounts (401k, IRA), real estate market value, vehicles, and valuable personal property. Liabilities: mortgage balance, car loans, student loans, credit card debt, personal loans, and any other obligations. Track quarterly. The trend — consistent growth — matters more than hitting specific numbers at specific ages.

Is it better to get a 15-year or 30-year mortgage?

A 15-year mortgage typically offers a rate 0.5-0.75% lower than a 30-year, builds equity faster, and saves dramatically on total interest. On a $300,000 loan at current rates, you might pay $250,000 in interest over 30 years versus $120,000 over 15 years — saving $130,000. The tradeoff: 15-year monthly payments are roughly 40-50% higher. Choose 30-year if the payment difference would strain your budget; choose 15-year if you can comfortably afford the higher payment and want to accelerate wealth building.
