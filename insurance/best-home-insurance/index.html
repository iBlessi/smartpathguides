What Does Homeowners Insurance Cover?

A standard HO-3 homeowners policy covers six categories of losses:

  • Dwelling (Coverage A): The physical structure of your home, including walls, roof, floors, and built-in appliances. Should equal the full replacement cost of your home — not its market value.
  • Other structures (Coverage B): Detached garages, fences, sheds, and other structures on your property. Typically 10% of dwelling coverage.
  • Personal property (Coverage C): Furniture, electronics, clothing, and other belongings inside (and sometimes outside) your home. Typically 50–70% of dwelling coverage.
  • Loss of use / additional living expenses (Coverage D): Hotel, restaurant, and other costs if you must temporarily live elsewhere while your home is being repaired after a covered loss.
  • Personal liability (Coverage E): Pays for legal costs and damages if someone is injured on your property or you accidentally damage someone else's property.
  • Medical payments (Coverage F): Pays minor medical bills for guests injured on your property, regardless of fault.
What Homeowners Insurance Does NOT Cover

Standard homeowners policies do not cover flood damage, earthquake damage, normal wear and tear, maintenance issues (like a leaky roof that hasn't been repaired), sewer backup (unless added as an endorsement), or high-value items like jewelry, art, or collectibles beyond standard sub-limits. Separate policies or endorsements are needed for these.

HO-3 vs. HO-5 Homeowners Insurance

When shopping for homeowners insurance, you'll encounter different policy forms. The two most common for single-family homes are HO-3 and HO-5.

HO-3 (Special Form)

An HO-3 is the most common homeowners policy. It covers your home's structure on an open-perils basis — meaning it covers all risks except those specifically excluded (like flood, earthquake, and normal wear). However, your personal property is covered on a named-perils basis — only the specific risks listed in your policy (fire, theft, vandalism, etc.).

HO-5 (Comprehensive Form)

An HO-5 provides the broadest protection. Both your home's structure and your personal property are covered on an open-perils basis. HO-5 policies also typically pay replacement cost rather than actual cash value for personal property losses. This means if your five-year-old laptop is stolen, an HO-5 pays what a new equivalent laptop costs — not the depreciated value of the old one.

HO-5 policies cost approximately 10–25% more than HO-3 policies. For most homeowners with significant personal property and a desire for maximum protection, the additional cost is worthwhile.

Flood Insurance: A Critical Gap

Standard homeowners insurance does not cover flooding — not from hurricanes, overflowing rivers, storm surge, or heavy rain. If your home floods without a separate flood policy, you bear the full cost of repairs out of pocket.

The primary source of flood insurance is the National Flood Insurance Program (NFIP), administered by FEMA. NFIP policies provide up to $250,000 in building coverage and $100,000 in contents coverage. Private flood insurance is increasingly available and can offer higher limits and broader coverage.

Flood insurance is mandatory if you have a federally backed mortgage and your home is in a FEMA-designated Special Flood Hazard Area (SFHA). But consider it even outside those zones — approximately 40% of NFIP flood claims come from properties outside high-risk flood zones.

Umbrella Insurance: Extended Liability Protection

A personal umbrella insurance policy provides liability coverage above and beyond what your homeowners and auto policies provide. If you're sued after a serious accident on your property and the judgment exceeds your $300,000 homeowners liability limit, an umbrella policy covers the excess — up to $1 million, $2 million, or more.

A $1 million umbrella policy typically costs $150–$300 per year. Given the potential exposure of significant liability lawsuits, umbrella insurance is one of the most cost-effective forms of protection available. It's particularly recommended for homeowners with swimming pools, trampolines, dogs, or other elevated liability risks.

How to Lower Your Homeowners Insurance Premium

  1. Bundle with auto insurance. Combining home and auto with the same insurer typically saves 5–25%. This is the single largest discount most homeowners can access.
  2. Raise your deductible. Increasing your deductible from $500 to $1,000 or $2,500 can reduce premiums by 10–30%. Keep the deductible in a savings account.
  3. Improve home security. Installing a monitored security system, smoke detectors, deadbolt locks, and storm shutters can qualify for discounts of 5–15%.
  4. Upgrade your roof. A new impact-resistant roof can significantly reduce your premium, especially in hail-prone states. Some insurers offer discounts of 20–40% for Class 4 rated roofing materials.
  5. Maintain good credit. In most states, credit is a major rating factor. Improving your credit score over time can substantially reduce your homeowners premium.
  6. Stay claims-free. Filing small claims can trigger surcharges that far exceed what you recover. For losses below $2,000–$3,000, paying out of pocket is often the financially smarter choice.
  7. Shop every 2–3 years. Rates shift, and loyalty doesn't always pay. Get competing quotes at renewal time — switching can save hundreds.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does homeowners insurance cost in 2026?
The national average is approximately $2,100–$2,400 per year for a $300,000 home with standard HO-3 coverage. Rates vary dramatically by location — Florida, Louisiana, Oklahoma, and Texas have much higher averages due to severe weather risk, while Oregon, Wisconsin, and Vermont tend to have lower rates.
What does homeowners insurance cover?
A standard HO-3 covers: your home's structure, other structures (garages, fences), personal property, additional living expenses if you must relocate after a covered loss, personal liability, and guest medical payments. It does NOT cover floods, earthquakes, normal wear and tear, or maintenance issues.
What is the difference between HO-3 and HO-5 policies?
HO-3 covers your home's structure on an open-perils basis but personal property only on a named-perils basis. HO-5 covers both structure and personal property on an open-perils basis and typically pays replacement cost for personal property. HO-5 costs about 10–25% more but provides significantly broader protection.
Does homeowners insurance cover floods?
No. Standard homeowners insurance does not cover flood damage. You need a separate flood insurance policy through the NFIP or a private insurer. Flood insurance is required by mortgage lenders for homes in high-risk flood zones and is worth considering even outside those zones.
What is an umbrella insurance policy?
An umbrella policy provides additional liability coverage above your homeowners and auto policy limits. A $1 million umbrella typically costs $150–$300 per year and is recommended for homeowners, especially those with pools, dogs, or other elevated liability exposure.
How can I lower my homeowners insurance premium?
Key strategies: bundle with auto insurance (5–25% savings), raise your deductible, install security systems, upgrade your roof, maintain good credit, stay claims-free for small losses, and shop competing quotes every 2–3 years.
What is replacement cost vs. actual cash value coverage?
Replacement cost pays what it costs to replace your damaged property with new items. Actual cash value pays the depreciated value of your old items. Replacement cost coverage is worth the extra premium — it prevents you from paying out of pocket to cover the depreciation gap on expensive items.

The Bottom Line

Homeowners insurance is not optional — your lender requires it, and without it, a single catastrophic event could wipe out your home equity. The key is to have the right coverage, not just the cheapest policy.

Amica consistently leads in customer satisfaction and should be your first quote if you're available in their service area. Erie offers outstanding value with guaranteed replacement cost included as standard. For military families, USAA is almost always the best choice. And if you want a seamless digital experience, Lemonade delivers a genuinely innovative claims and policy management process.

Regardless of which insurer you choose, make sure your dwelling coverage reflects your home's actual rebuild cost — not its market value or purchase price. Review your coverage annually as construction costs have risen sharply and many homeowners are dangerously underinsured.
